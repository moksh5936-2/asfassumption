# Architecture 4 – Implicit Security Assumptions (GPT-4o, Fresh Run)

Assumption 1: Patient devices are trusted

Why it matters:
Patients access PHI through the portal using personal devices.

If false:
Malware may steal credentials, session tokens, or PHI displayed in the portal.

⸻

Assumption 2: Patient Portal properly validates user identity

Why it matters:
The portal serves as the first security boundary.

If false:
Unauthorized individuals may gain access to patient records.

⸻

Assumption 3: Auth0 is securely configured

Why it matters:
Authentication security depends heavily on identity provider configuration.

If false:
Authentication bypasses or weak access controls may occur.

⸻

Assumption 4: Auth0 infrastructure is secure

Why it matters:
Auth0 functions as a trust anchor.

If false:
Compromise could affect every authenticated user.

⸻

Assumption 5: Administrative access to Auth0 is protected

Why it matters:
Identity administrators can alter authentication behavior.

If false:
Attackers could create users, reset passwords, or bypass controls.

⸻

Assumption 6: Strong authentication methods are used

Why it matters:
The architecture does not explicitly mention MFA.

If false:
Credential theft may be sufficient to access PHI.

⸻

Assumption 7: Password recovery processes are secure

Why it matters:
Recovery workflows frequently become attack targets.

If false:
Attackers may gain accounts through social engineering.

⸻

Assumption 8: TLS protects Portal-to-Application communication

Why it matters:
PHI frequently traverses this boundary.

If false:
Sensitive patient data may be intercepted.

⸻

Assumption 9: TLS certificates are trusted and valid

Why it matters:
Encrypted communication relies on certificate trust.

If false:
Man-in-the-middle attacks become possible.

⸻

Assumption 10: Session management is secure

Why it matters:
Authentication is typically represented by sessions or tokens.

If false:
Session hijacking may occur.

⸻

Assumption 11: Session identifiers are unpredictable

Why it matters:
Weak session entropy enables guessing attacks.

If false:
Unauthorized users may impersonate patients.

⸻

Assumption 12: Session expiration is properly enforced

Why it matters:
Long-lived sessions increase risk.

If false:
Stolen sessions may remain valid indefinitely.

⸻

Assumption 13: Application authorization is correctly implemented

Why it matters:
Patients should only access their own PHI.

If false:
Patients may access records belonging to others.

⸻

Assumption 14: Object-level authorization is enforced

Why it matters:
Healthcare applications commonly expose records by identifier.

If false:
IDOR vulnerabilities may expose PHI.

⸻

Assumption 15: Minimum necessary access is correctly implemented

Why it matters:
The policy states it is enforced.

If false:
Users may view more PHI than necessary.

⸻

Assumption 16: Employees receive only necessary permissions

Why it matters:
Internal users often have broad access opportunities.

If false:
Insider misuse becomes easier.

⸻

Assumption 17: Privileged access is tightly controlled

Administrators often possess broad PHI access.

If false:
Compromise may expose the entire patient database.

⸻

Assumption 18: User provisioning is accurate

Why it matters:
Healthcare access decisions depend on role assignments.

If false:
Unauthorized access may occur.

⸻

Assumption 19: User deprovisioning is timely

Why it matters:
Departed employees should lose access immediately.

If false:
Former personnel may continue accessing PHI.

⸻

Assumption 20: Application input validation is effective

Why it matters:
Users control application inputs.

If false:
Injection attacks may occur.

⸻

Assumption 21: Database queries are parameterized

Why it matters:
The application directly accesses PHI.

If false:
SQL injection may expose patient records.

Assumption 22: App servers are hardened

Why it matters:
Application compromise can expose PHI.

If false:
Attackers may gain backend access.

Assumption 23: Secrets are securely managed

Attackers may impersonate systems.
