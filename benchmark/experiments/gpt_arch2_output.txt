# Architecture 2 – Implicit Security Assumptions (GPT-4o, Fresh Run)

Assumption 1: User devices are trusted

Why it matters:
The architecture assumes browsers and endpoints used to access Okta are not compromised.

If false:
Malware could steal sessions, credentials, MFA tokens, or SAML assertions.

⸻

Assumption 2: Browsers properly validate TLS certificates

Why it matters:
Authentication begins at the browser-to-IdP boundary.

If false:
Users may submit credentials to attacker-controlled systems.

⸻

Assumption 3: TLS is correctly configured between browsers and Okta

Why it matters:
SAML authentication relies on secure transport.

If false:
Credentials or session tokens may be intercepted.

⸻

Assumption 4: Okta infrastructure is secure

Why it matters:
Okta acts as the central identity authority.

If false:
Compromise of the IdP compromises every connected application.

⸻

Assumption 5: Administrative access to Okta is protected

Why it matters:
IdP administrators control authentication policy.

If false:
Attackers could modify authentication rules or create accounts.

⸻

Assumption 6: MFA methods are resistant to phishing

Why it matters:
The policy only states MFA is enforced.

If false:
Attackers may bypass MFA using phishing kits or adversary-in-the-middle attacks.

⸻

Assumption 7: MFA enrollment is secure

Why it matters:
Weak enrollment procedures undermine MFA.

If false:
Attackers may register their own devices.

⸻

Assumption 8: MFA recovery procedures are secure

Why it matters:
Account recovery often bypasses normal authentication.

If false:
Attackers may take over accounts through help-desk abuse.

⸻

Assumption 9: Authentication policies are correctly configured

Why it matters:
MFA enforcement depends on policy implementation.

If false:
Certain users or applications may bypass MFA.

⸻

Assumption 10: SAML assertions are digitally signed

Why it matters:
Service providers must trust assertions from the IdP.

If false:
Attackers may forge identities.

⸻

Assumption 11: Service providers verify SAML signatures

Why it matters:
Signing alone is insufficient.

If false:
Forged assertions may be accepted.

⸻

Assumption 12: Service providers validate assertion expiration

Why it matters:
Assertions should have limited lifetime.

If false:
Replay attacks become possible.

⸻

Assumption 13: Service providers validate audience restrictions

Why it matters:
Assertions should only be usable by intended applications.

If false:
An assertion issued for one application may be accepted by another.

⸻

Assumption 14: Service providers validate issuer identity

Why it matters:
Applications must confirm assertions originated from Okta.

If false:
Malicious IdPs could be trusted.

⸻

Assumption 15: SAML certificates are managed securely

Why it matters:
Federation trust depends on certificates.

If false:
Attackers may forge authentication assertions.

⸻

Assumption 16: Certificate rotation occurs correctly

Why it matters:
Certificates eventually expire.

If false:
Authentication failures or trust issues may occur.

⸻

Assumption 17: Clock synchronization is accurate

Why it matters:
SAML relies heavily on timestamps.

If false:
Valid assertions may be rejected or expired assertions accepted.

⸻

Assumption 18: AD is secure

Why it matters:
AD serves as the authoritative user store.

If false:
Attackers could create users or elevate privileges.

⸻

Assumption 19: Directory synchronization is trustworthy

Why it matters:
Identity information flows from AD to Okta.

If false:
Unauthorized accounts may propagate.

⸻

Assumption 20: Synchronization channels are protected

Why it matters:
Directory data contains sensitive identity information.

If false:
Attackers may tamper with user records.

⸻

Assumption 21: User provisioning logic is correct

Why it matters:
JIT provisioning automatically creates accounts.

If false:
Improper access rights may be granted.

⸻

Assumption 22: JIT provisioning only creates legitimate users

Why it matters:
Automatic account creation expands trust.

If false:
Attackers may obtain application accounts.

⸻

Assumption 23: JIT provisioning assigns appropriate roles

Why it matters:
Provisioning affects authorization.

If false:
Users may receive excessive permissions.

⸻

Assumption 24: User deprovisioning is timely

Why it matters:
Access should end when employment ends.

If false:
Former employees retain access.

⸻

Assumption 25: Group memberships are accurate

Why it matters:
Authorization often depends on group assignments.

If false:
Users may gain unauthorized privileges.

⸻

Assumption 26: Service providers enforce authorization independently

Why it matters:
Authentication does not equal authorization.

If false:
Any authenticated user may gain excessive access.

⸻

Assumption 27: Session timeout is enforced consistently

Why it matters:
Policy specifies 8-hour timeout.

If false:
Sessions may persist indefinitely.

⸻

Assumption 28: Session termination invalidates tokens

Why it matters:
Timeouts must actually revoke access.

If false:
Stolen sessions remain usable.

⸻

Assumption 29: Session identifiers are unpredictable

Why it matters:
Session security depends on entropy.

If false:
Sessions may be guessed or forged.

⸻

Assumption 30: Session storage is secure

Why it matters:
Browsers store authentication state.

If false:
Attackers may steal active sessions.

⸻

Assumption 31: Single Logout works correctly

Why it matters:
Users expect logout to end access everywhere.

If false:
Applications may remain accessible after logout.

⸻

Assumption 32: Applications correctly consume SAML attributes

Why it matters:
Authorization often depends on attributes.

If false:
Users may receive incorrect permissions.

⸻

Assumption 33: Applications do not trust unvalidated attributes

Why it matters:
Attribute tampering can affect authorization.

If false:
Privilege escalation may occur.

⸻

Assumption 34: Federation metadata is accurate

Why it matters:
Trust relationships depend on metadata.

If false:
Applications may trust incorrect endpoints.

⸻

Assumption 35: Federation metadata updates are authenticated

Why it matters:
Metadata controls trust anchors.

If false:
Attackers could redirect authentication flows.

⸻

Assumption 36: Administrative actions are logged

Why it matters:
Identity systems require accountability.

If false:
Malicious changes may go undetected.

⸻

Assumption 37: Logs cannot be altered

Why it matters:
Audit records must remain trustworthy.

If false:
Attackers may erase evidence.

⸻

Assumption 38: Monitoring detects identity attacks

Why it matters:
SSO systems are high-value targets.

If false:
Credential abuse may persist undetected.

⸻

Assumption 39: Help-desk processes are secure

Why it matters:
Identity recovery often involves support staff.

If false:
Social engineering may bypass authentication controls.

⸻

Assumption 40: Insider administrators are trustworthy

Why it matters:
Identity administrators control access to every application.

If false:
A single insider may compromise the entire environment.

⸻

Assumption 41: Service providers are individually secure

Why it matters:
SSO centralizes authentication but not application security.

If false:
Compromise of one application may expose sensitive data.

⸻

Assumption 42: No application has a local authentication bypass

Why it matters:
Policy states all apps require SSO.

If false:
Attackers may bypass federation entirely.

⸻

Assumption 43: Legacy protocols are disabled

Why it matters:
Older protocols may bypass MFA.

If false:
Attackers may exploit weaker authentication paths.

⸻

Assumption 44: Identity data integrity is preserved

Why it matters:
Authorization decisions depend on accurate identity information.

If false:
Privilege assignments become unreliable.

⸻

Assumption 45: No undocumented trust relationships exist

Why it matters:
Architectural diagrams rarely show every federation connection.

If false:
Hidden trust paths may bypass intended controls.
