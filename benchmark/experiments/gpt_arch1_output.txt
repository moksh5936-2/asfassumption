# Architecture 1 – Implicit Security Assumptions (GPT-4o, Fresh Run)

Assumption 1: User laptops are trusted endpoints

Why it matters:
The architecture assumes the laptop connecting through the VPN is not compromised by malware, spyware, keyloggers, or remote-access trojans.

If false:
An attacker could steal AD credentials, hijack VPN sessions, access the payroll application, and potentially retrieve sensitive payroll data despite all documented controls.

⸻

Assumption 2: VPN authentication is strong enough to resist credential theft

Why it matters:
The documented policy only states that VPN access is required. It does not specify MFA, certificate authentication, or phishing resistance.

If false:
Stolen usernames and passwords may be sufficient for remote access.

⸻

Assumption 3: VPN users are properly authenticated before access is granted

Why it matters:
The VPN gateway serves as the first trust boundary.

If false:
Unauthorized users could enter the internal network and attack internal systems.

⸻

Assumption 4: VPN gateway software is not vulnerable

Why it matters:
The VPN gateway is a highly exposed security-critical component.

If false:
Attackers could bypass authentication entirely or gain internal network access.

⸻

Assumption 5: VPN gateway configuration is secure

Why it matters:
Even secure software can be insecurely configured.

If false:
Misconfigurations could expose internal resources directly.

⸻

Assumption 6: TLS is correctly configured between VPN and application

Why it matters:
TLS only provides security when implemented correctly.

If false:
Traffic interception, modification, or credential theft may occur.

⸻

Assumption 7: Certificates used by TLS are trusted

Why it matters:
TLS trust depends on certificate validity.

If false:
Man-in-the-middle attacks become possible.

⸻

Assumption 8: Internal DNS is trustworthy

Why it matters:
Users and systems rely on DNS resolution.

If false:
Traffic could be redirected to attacker-controlled systems.

⸻

Assumption 9: Active Directory is secure

Why it matters:
Application authentication depends on AD.

If false:
Attackers could create accounts, elevate privileges, or impersonate users.

⸻

Assumption 10: AD credentials are protected

Why it matters:
Passwords represent the primary identity proof.

If false:
Payroll data may be exposed through credential compromise.

⸻

Assumption 11: AD authorization groups are correctly maintained

Why it matters:
Authentication alone does not determine access rights.

If false:
Unauthorized employees may gain payroll access.

⸻

Assumption 12: User accounts are deprovisioned promptly

Why it matters:
Former employees should lose access immediately.

If false:
Ex-employees may retain payroll system access.

⸻

Assumption 13: Service accounts are protected

Why it matters:
Applications often use privileged accounts.

If false:
Attackers may gain backend access.

⸻

Assumption 14: Application authorization is implemented correctly

Why it matters:
Authenticated users should only access permitted functions.

If false:
Privilege escalation may occur.

⸻

Assumption 15: Session management is secure

Why it matters:
Applications rely on sessions after authentication.

If false:
Session hijacking becomes possible.

⸻

Assumption 16: Session identifiers are unpredictable

Why it matters:
Weak session IDs can be guessed.

If false:
Attackers may impersonate legitimate users.

⸻

Assumption 17: Application input validation is effective

Why it matters:
User input is attacker-controlled.

If false:
Injection attacks become possible.

⸻

Assumption 18: SQL queries are parameterized

Why it matters:
The application directly accesses the database.

If false:
SQL injection could expose payroll records.

⸻

Assumption 19: Application servers are hardened

Why it matters:
Compromised servers undermine all higher-level controls.

If false:
Attackers may gain direct database access.

⸻

Assumption 20: Administrative interfaces are restricted

Why it matters:
Management functions are highly privileged.

If false:
Attackers could take over the application.

⸻

Assumption 21: Secrets are securely stored

Why it matters:
Applications require credentials and keys.

If false:
Attackers may impersonate systems.

⸻

Assumption 22: Database credentials are protected

Why it matters:
The application must authenticate to the database.

If false:
Direct database access becomes possible.

⸻

Assumption 23: The private subnet is actually isolated

Why it matters:
The documented policy states the DB is private.

If false:
External attackers may directly reach the database.

⸻

Assumption 24: Firewall rules are correct

Why it matters:
Network segmentation relies on them.

If false:
Unauthorized network paths may exist.

⸻

Assumption 25: Only the application can access the database

Why it matters:
Trust boundary enforcement depends on this.

If false:
Attackers may bypass application controls.

⸻

Assumption 26: Database permissions follow least privilege

Why it matters:
Applications rarely require full admin rights.

If false:
Application compromise yields excessive access.

⸻

Assumption 27: Database software is patched

Why it matters:
Known vulnerabilities are routinely exploited.

If false:
Attackers may compromise payroll records.

⸻

Assumption 28: Database encryption keys are protected

Why it matters:
Encryption is only as secure as the keys.

If false:
Encrypted data may be readable.

⸻

Assumption 29: Backups are protected

Why it matters:
Backups often contain full payroll datasets.

If false:
Attackers may steal data from backups.

⸻

Assumption 30: Backup access is restricted

Why it matters:
Backup repositories are attractive targets.

If false:
Data leakage may occur outside production systems.

⸻

Assumption 31: Backup integrity is verified

Why it matters:
Nightly backups are only useful if valid.

If false:
Recovery may fail after an incident.

⸻

Assumption 32: Logs cannot be altered by attackers

Why it matters:
Incident investigation depends on trustworthy logs.

If false:
Evidence may be destroyed.

⸻

Assumption 33: Security monitoring exists

Why it matters:
Controls are ineffective if compromise goes unnoticed.

If false:
Attackers may persist for long periods.

⸻

Assumption 34: Time synchronization is accurate

Why it matters:
Authentication and logging rely on timestamps.

If false:
Incident analysis becomes unreliable.

⸻

Assumption 35: Administrators are trustworthy

Why it matters:
Privileged insiders can bypass controls.

If false:
Payroll data may be intentionally abused.

⸻

Assumption 36: Change management prevents insecure modifications

Why it matters:
Security posture changes over time.

If false:
Controls may silently weaken.

⸻

Assumption 37: Physical security protects infrastructure

Why it matters:
Physical access often defeats logical controls.

If false:
Systems may be directly compromised.

⸻

Assumption 38: Cloud provider isolation works correctly (if hosted)

Why it matters:
Multi-tenant environments rely on isolation.

If false:
Cross-tenant compromise becomes possible.

⸻

Assumption 39: Network devices enforce policy correctly

Why it matters:
Segmentation depends on routers, switches, and firewalls.

If false:
Unauthorized communication paths may emerge.

⸻

Assumption 40: No undocumented access paths exist

Why it matters:
Architectural diagrams rarely capture everything.

If false:
Attackers may exploit hidden routes around security controls.
