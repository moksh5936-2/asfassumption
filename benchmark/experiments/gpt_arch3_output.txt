# Architecture 3 – Implicit Security Assumptions (GPT-4o, Fresh Run)

Assumption 1: Kubernetes worker nodes are trusted

Why it matters:
All workloads ultimately run on cluster nodes.

If false:
A compromised node may bypass mTLS, steal secrets, inspect traffic, and compromise workloads.

⸻

Assumption 2: The Kubernetes control plane is secure

Why it matters:
The control plane governs cluster-wide security.

If false:
An attacker could modify workloads, permissions, certificates, or networking policies.

⸻

Assumption 3: etcd is protected

Why it matters:
etcd stores cluster state, secrets, RBAC definitions, and configuration.

If false:
Attackers may gain full cluster compromise.

⸻

Assumption 4: etcd access is restricted

Why it matters:
Unauthorized access to etcd is effectively cluster-admin access.

If false:
Sensitive information and credentials may be exposed.

⸻

Assumption 5: etcd data integrity is maintained

Why it matters:
The cluster depends on trusted configuration data.

If false:
Attackers could manipulate permissions and workloads.

⸻

Assumption 6: Citadel CA is trustworthy

Why it matters:
The entire mesh trust model depends on the CA.

If false:
Attackers may issue fraudulent service certificates.

⸻

Assumption 7: CA private keys are protected

Why it matters:
CA compromise breaks service identity.

If false:
Any service can be impersonated.

⸻

Assumption 8: Certificate issuance is properly controlled

Why it matters:
Only legitimate workloads should receive certificates.

If false:
Malicious workloads may join the mesh.

⸻

Assumption 9: Certificate revocation works effectively

Why it matters:
Compromised identities must be removed quickly.

If false:
Attackers may continue using stolen certificates.

⸻

Assumption 10: Certificate rotation functions correctly

Why it matters:
mTLS relies on valid certificates.

If false:
Expired or compromised certificates may remain trusted.

⸻

Assumption 11: Istio Pilot is trustworthy

Why it matters:
Pilot distributes service mesh configuration.

If false:
Traffic may be redirected or intercepted.

⸻

Assumption 12: Pilot configuration is accurate

Why it matters:
Security policies are propagated through Pilot.

If false:
Services may communicate in unintended ways.

⸻

Assumption 13: Sidecar proxies cannot be bypassed

Why it matters:
mTLS enforcement occurs through sidecars.

If false:
Traffic may travel outside the mesh unprotected.

⸻

Assumption 14: All service traffic actually traverses the mesh

Why it matters:
The policy only states mTLS is enabled.

If false:
Some traffic may be unencrypted.

⸻

Assumption 15: Service identities are unique

Why it matters:
mTLS authorization depends on identity.

If false:
Identity confusion may occur.

⸻

Assumption 16: Service certificates correctly represent workloads

Why it matters:
Authorization decisions depend on identity accuracy.

If false:
Unauthorized services may gain access.

⸻

Assumption 17: mTLS is enforced rather than optional

Why it matters:
Some meshes support permissive modes.

If false:
Services may accept plaintext connections.

⸻

Assumption 18: Services validate peer certificates

Why it matters:
Mutual TLS requires validation by both sides.

If false:
Impersonation attacks become possible.

⸻

Assumption 19: Ingress Gateway is hardened

Why it matters:
It is the primary external attack surface.

If false:
Attackers may gain entry into the cluster.

⸻

Assumption 20: Ingress routing rules are secure

Why it matters:
Routing determines traffic destinations.

If false:
Requests may reach unauthorized services.

⸻

Assumption 21: Kubernetes API authentication is secure

Why it matters:
The API controls the cluster.

If false:
Unauthorized users may gain administrative access.

⸻

Assumption 22: Kubernetes API authorization is correct

Why it matters:
Authentication alone is insufficient.

If false:
Users may obtain excessive privileges.

⸻

Assumption 23: Service accounts are protected

Why it matters:
Pods commonly authenticate using service accounts.

If false:
Attackers may impersonate workloads.

⸻

Assumption 24: Service account tokens cannot be stolen

Why it matters:
Tokens grant cluster permissions.

If false:
Cluster compromise may occur.

⸻

Assumption 25: Namespace RBAC is correctly implemented

Why it matters:
The documented policy relies on namespace RBAC.

If false:
Users may access resources outside intended boundaries.

⸻

Assumption 26: Cluster-admin privileges are tightly controlled

Why it matters:
Cluster-admin bypasses all namespace controls.

If false:
A single compromise may affect the entire environment.

⸻

Assumption 27: Pod Security Policies (or equivalent controls) are enforced

Why it matters:
Policy assumes privileged containers are restricted.

If false:
Containers may gain host-level access.

⸻

Assumption 28: Containers cannot escape to the host

Why it matters:
Container isolation is fundamental.

If false:
Attackers may compromise worker nodes.

⸻

Assumption 29: Container runtimes are secure

Why it matters:
Runtime vulnerabilities affect isolation.

If false:
Workloads may break containment.

⸻

Assumption 30: Images are trusted

Why it matters:
Applications originate from container images.

If false:
Malicious code may enter production.

⸻

Assumption 31: Image registries are secure

Why it matters:
Registries distribute workloads.

If false:
Attackers may introduce malicious images.

⸻

Assumption 32: Network policies are correctly defined

Why it matters:
The architecture relies on namespace isolation.

If false:
Unexpected communication paths may exist.

⸻

Assumption 33: Network policies are actually enforced

Why it matters:
Definition alone is insufficient.

If false:
Isolation may not exist in practice.

⸻

Assumption 34: No alternate networking path bypasses policies

Why it matters:
Pods may communicate through unintended routes.

If false:
Security boundaries may be circumvented.

⸻

Assumption 35: DNS inside the cluster is trustworthy

Why it matters:
Service discovery depends on DNS.

If false:
Traffic may be redirected to malicious services.

⸻

Assumption 36: Secrets management is secure

Why it matters:
Services require credentials and keys.

If false:
Attackers may gain unauthorized access.

⸻

Assumption 37: Secrets are encrypted when stored

Why it matters:
Kubernetes secrets are not inherently secure.

If false:
Credentials may be exposed.

⸻

Assumption 38: StatefulSet database permissions are least privilege

Why it matters:
Applications rarely require full database control.

If false:
Application compromise yields excessive impact.

⸻

Assumption 39: Persistent Volumes are protected

Why it matters:
Data survives pod restarts.

If false:
Attackers may access sensitive information directly.

⸻

Assumption 40: Persistent Volume snapshots are secure

Why it matters:
Snapshots often contain complete datasets.

If false:
Sensitive data may leak outside the cluster.

⸻

Assumption 41: Monitoring systems are trustworthy

Why it matters:
Detection depends on accurate telemetry.

If false:
Attacks may go unnoticed.

⸻

Assumption 42: Logs cannot be modified by attackers

Why it matters:
Forensics require trustworthy evidence.

If false:
Attackers may erase traces of compromise.

⸻

Assumption 43: Time synchronization is accurate

Why it matters:
Certificates, logs, and tokens depend on time.

If false:
Authentication failures and forensic confusion may occur.

⸻

Assumption 44: Admission controllers prevent unauthorized workloads

Why it matters:
Workload deployment is a critical trust point.

If false:
Malicious pods may enter the cluster.

⸻

Assumption 45: Operators and administrators are trustworthy

Why it matters:
Privileged users control the environment.

If false:
Insider threats may bypass technical controls.

⸻

Assumption 46: No hidden cluster-wide permissions exist

Why it matters:
Complex RBAC environments often accumulate privilege.

If false:
Attackers may exploit unintended access paths.

⸻

Assumption 47: Service A trusts only authorized callers

Why it matters:
Identity alone should not imply authorization.

If false:
Any authenticated workload may gain access.

⸻

Assumption 48: Service B enforces authorization decisions

Why it matters:
mTLS proves identity, not permission.

If false:
Lateral movement becomes easier.

⸻

Assumption 49: Service C protects sensitive operations

Why it matters:
Backend services often hold critical data.

If false:
Attackers may access protected resources.

⸻

Assumption 50: No undocumented trust relationships exist

Why it matters:
Real-world clusters are often more complex than diagrams.

If false:
Attackers may exploit hidden communication paths that bypass intended security controls.
